Marc Andreessen is calling for a "Space Age Renaissance" — and I'm all for the ambition. But renaissance requires infrastructure, not just vision. This week's GAO report on the Sentinel ICBM program is a case study in what happens when we let physical plant decay: software delays are now compounding on top of a requirement to replace over 600 facilities across five states. Until we treat facility modernization and regional production capacity as first-order national security investments — not line items to defer — we'll keep watching schedules slip and costs balloon. Post 3  [inspired by: Schmidt's capital constraints framing + Northrop solid rocket motor supplier needs + Agile Space Series A]
------------------------------------------------------
Eric Schmidt raised an important question this week: what happens when scaling AI models hits capital constraints and companies have to differentiate on something other than sheer compute? The same logic applies to aerospace and defense manufacturing right now. Northrop Grumman's CEO just said the company won't seek direct DoD investment for solid rocket motor production — but acknowledged suppliers further down the chain may need government support. This is the real bottleneck. Primes can self-fund at scale. Tier 2 and Tier 3 suppliers — the shops doing specialty welding, precision machining, energetics handling — often cannot. Agile Space Industries closing an oversubscribed $17M Series A for in-space propulsion shows private capital will flow to manufacturing when the unit economics are clear. But most defense suppliers don't have that luxury. If we're serious about scaling the industrial base for both commercial space and national security, we need financing mechanisms that reach the sub-tier. The constraint isn't demand. It's distributed production capacity at the supplier level. #DefenseIndustrial #SpaceManufacturing #SupplyChain
